Some Philosophy

These opinions are strictly my own and are subject to change.
  • Quality is more important than quantity. Pick a handful of competitions and focus on them.
  • A hard question is not necessarily a good question. A common misstep of newer writers is a tendency to ask about obscure phenomena or advanced topics, which only discourages less experienced students taking the exam.
  • The good questions can be solved with knowing very little, but applying that bit of knowledge cleverly.
  • If an exam takes fewer than twelve hours to write, odds are there's a lot of room to improve.
  • Ideally, an exam average will be about 50%, with minimum 20% and maximum 80%.

Grading Tips

  • During in-person competitions, I find it helpful to keep a question-by-question record of each team. A sample spreadsheet populated with random data for 2020 Yale Machines can be found here. It takes time to do this at a competition, but some advantages include:
    • I now can see which questions teams generally solved and those with which they had difficulty.
    • Emperically, mis-addition is the most common error when grading. Having a spreadsheet makes it possible to cross check the manually calculated totals with those from the spreadsheet. In my experience, this catches most (if not all) addition errors.
  • Always grade question-by-question if time allows. This ensures fair grading for all teams.
  • Make a rubric ahead of time for short answer and open response questions (see 2020 Yale Machines for an example).
